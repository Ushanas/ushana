These turtles are hunted to be harvested for their unique shell, which is often used in decorative items, jewelry, and souvenirs. Hawksbill turtles also face threats posed by habitat loss and pollution in their marine habitats.

    Moreover, Sri Lanka hosts the largest nesting colony of Leatherback Turtles in the Indian Ocean region. However, these turtles are also on the list for critically endangered species. Leatherback turtle populations are threatened by commercial fishing practices and climate change, which could change ocean currents, impacting their nesting success. The fishing practices that use longlines and gillnets also contribute to the bycatch of endangered leatherback turtles, with many getting entangled and drowning.

    Conservation efforts in Sri Lanka

    Turtle conservation efforts in Sri Lanka have been ongoing for many years, with the aim of protecting the country's sea turtles and their habitats. Sri Lanka is home to several species of sea turtles, including the green turtle, the hawksbill turtle, and the leatherback turtle. These species are threatened by a variety of factors, including habitat destruction, poaching, and pollution.

    One of the main conservation efforts in Sri Lanka is the protection of turtle nesting sites. Many of Sri Lanka's beaches are used as nesting sites by sea turtles, and conservation efforts are focused on protecting these areas from disturbance. This includes the establishment of protected areas and the implementation of beach patrols to prevent illegal activities such as poaching and habitat destruction.

This includes efforts to prevent pollution and to protect coral reefs and other marine habitats that sea turtles rely on.     Challenges faced by turtle population

    here are several challenges faced by Sri Lankan turtle populations that threaten their survival. Some of the main challenges include

    • Habitat destruction


    • Poaching


    • Climate change


    • Pollution


    Habitat destruction: Sri Lanka's beaches are a vital habitat for sea turtles, but they are also under threat from human activities such as coastal development and pollution. The destruction of nesting sites and the degradation of marine habitats can have a significant impact on turtle populations.

    Poaching: Sea turtles are often targeted by poachers who capture them for their meat, shells, and eggs. This illegal trade can have a devastating impact on turtle populations, particularly if it is not adequately addressed.

    Climate change: is also a significant threat to Sri Lankan turtle populations. Rising sea temperatures and changes in ocean currents can alter the timing of turtle nesting and migration, which can have a significant impact on their survival

    Pollution: is another major threat to Sri Lankan turtle populations. Plastic pollution, in particular, can be deadly for turtles, as they can mistake plastic debris for food and ingest it, leading to injury or death
